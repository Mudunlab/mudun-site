That's not really the point of this piece. What stayed with me was the process: standing on a street, working out exactly what's wrong with it, and figuring out what a cheap, temporary fix could look like. That's what I want to walk through here.

  ### Test it before you build it

  Tactical urbanism is the practice of trying a street redesign with cheap, temporary materials before committing to permanent construction. Paint instead of new asphalt. Planters and bollards instead of poured concrete curbs. Recycled tires instead of a real median. If the layout works, you build it properly later. If it doesn't, you move the planters and try something else.

  But testing isn't the only reason cities do this. A temporary intervention is also a way of making an argument. It's much easier to convince people a redesign works when they can walk through it, than when they're looking at a drawing of it. That applies to the residents who'll actually use the street day to day — and it applies just as much to whoever eventually has to sign off on spending real money to make it permanent. A soft launch does the convincing that a proposal document can't.

  That's the lens I want to apply below: not "here's a nice street redesign," but here's what I was actually looking for, and why it mattered enough to act on.

  ***

  ### When a street's width doesn't match its job

  The first thing I check on any street is whether its width is doing the job it's supposed to be doing. On this corridor, the lower stretch runs one-way, uphill, past a school, and it's roughly ten meters across — wide enough for two cars to sit side by side and overtake. On paper that's unremarkable. On the ground, it means cars are overtaking exactly where kids are standing before and after class.

  This kind of mismatch matters because width is one of the few things drivers read instinctively. Nobody needs a sign to know a road is wide enough to speed up in. So the fix wasn't signage — it was taking the width away. Narrow the carriageway to a single lane, reclaim a meter and a half to two meters on the pedestrian side, and at the school itself, remove parking entirely and turn that space into a real waiting zone: a marked crossing, bench seating, shade sails over where people already stand. At the smaller junctions along the same stretch, painted curb extensions and planters brought crossing distances down from about fifteen meters to seven or nine — the difference between a crossing you glance at and one you have to commit to.

  ### When people have already decided what a space is for

  The second thing I look for is evidence that a space is already being used for something, whether or not it was designed for it. That's usually a stronger signal than anything on a plan, because it's free data — nobody had to be surveyed to produce it.

  The clearest case on this corridor was a triangular traffic island partway up — fenced off, raised half a meter above the street, technically off-limits. And yet kids were playing football on the dirt inside the fence. People were sitting around its edges. The street had already decided what this space was for; the design just hadn't caught up.

  Once you see that, the intervention almost designs itself: stop fighting the use and build for it. Reclaim the excess road around the island as protected space, open the inside instead of fencing it shut, let the whole footprint roughly double — about 500 to 1,000 square meters. Keep the outer ring calmer, with recycled timber seating and planters for sitting and talking, and keep the inner island active — leveled ground, football, swings, the same fence but cut open at a few points so it works as a threshold instead of a wall. Add ramps at the crossing points so it's actually reachable, not just visible.

  The general rule I took from this: don't ask what a space could become. Ask what it's already become, and design for that properly.

  ***

  ### When geometry, not speed, is the danger

  The third check is on junctions specifically, and it's less about how fast people are driving than about the shape of the space they're driving through. Further up the corridor, one junction stood out as the single most dangerous point I documented: a twenty-meter crossing, cars parked on both sides killing sightlines, an entry angle shaped to encourage a fast turn rather than a slow one, and no marked crossing at all on a two-way street.

  ![](/uploads/13.jpg)

  ![](/uploads/14.jpg)

  ![](/uploads/15.jpg)

  A speed limit sign does nothing about geometry like that. What does something is changing the geometry itself: curb extensions to shorten the crossing and remove the parking that was blocking sightlines, a painted crosswalk, a refuge median so a pedestrian only has to read one direction of traffic at a time.

  Right beside that same junction, on a small flat patch with a few trees and a view across the city, kids had already turned an unused corner into a hangout spot — the same "already decided" signal as the island, just smaller. A few benches, planters, shade overhead. Nothing invented, just made to last through summer.

  ***

  ### When comfort decides whether a space gets used at all

  The last thing I check, especially in Amman, is heat. A technically safe street that's unshaded for long stretches still won't get used the way it should — people will take a car instead of a five-minute walk if that walk means direct sun with nowhere to stop. Near the mosque at the top of the corridor, and along the exposed stretch leading to it, the fix was almost entirely about comfort: lean-rails against the property walls, shade sails at intervals, a marked crossing at the mosque frontage itself where people already gather before and after prayers.

  ![](/uploads/16.jpg)

  The same logic applied to five stairways connecting the corridor down into the neighborhood — currently just functional, nothing else, but already used informally by kids as gathering spots. Painted risers, a bit of seating tucked to one side, shade above, still wide enough to pass through. And blank walls along the police station perimeter and elsewhere were slated for murals made with local artists, schools, and kids from the area — the one intervention here that isn't about safety or comfort at all, just about a street that looks like it belongs to the people living on it.

  ***

  ### The point of doing it this way

  None of this required new infrastructure to test. Paint, planters, bollards, a few benches — all reversible, all cheap enough to try before anyone commits real money. And that's really the case tactical urbanism makes for itself: it's much harder to argue with a street that's already working than with a rendering of one. That's true for the people who'll use it every day, and it's true for whoever eventually decides whether it becomes permanent.

  If there's one habit worth taking from this, it's the one underneath every example above: before designing anything, go and look at what a street has already become. Half the intervention is usually already there, waiting to be made official.
